Information Security Governance
17% of exam · Establish & maintain framework aligning security with business goals
Core Concept

Governance Framework

  • Security strategy must align with business objectives
  • Board/executive accountability for information security
  • Policies, standards, procedures, guidelines hierarchy
  • Security governance ≠ security management (strategic vs operational)
  • CISO reports to: ideally CEO or Board, not CIO
Key Roles

Responsibilities & Accountability

  • Information Owner: classifies data, approves access
  • Data Custodian: implements controls, day-to-day protection
  • CISM: advises, manages program, reports to senior mgmt
  • Board: ultimate accountability for governance
  • Risk Acceptance: always done by business management
Frameworks & Standards

Key Frameworks to Know

  • COBIT: IT governance, control objectives
  • ISO 27001: ISMS requirements (certifiable)
  • ISO 27002: controls guidance/best practices
  • NIST CSF: Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover
  • ITIL: IT service management
Policy Hierarchy

Policy → Standard → Procedure

  • Policy: high-level, management-approved, mandatory
  • Standard: specific requirements that support policy
  • Procedure: step-by-step instructions
  • Guideline: recommended, not mandatory
  • Baseline: minimum acceptable security level
Metrics & Reporting

Measuring Governance Effectiveness

  • KRI: Key Risk Indicator (early warning signals)
  • KPI: Key Performance Indicator (measure outcomes)
  • KGI: Key Goal Indicator (measure objectives met)
  • Report to Board: business risk terms, not technical
  • Balanced Scorecard: aligns security with business strategy
Exam Traps

Watch Out For

  • Risk acceptance is ALWAYS a business decision
  • Security strategy flows FROM business strategy
  • Governance is about oversight, not implementation
  • "First step" = usually understand current state / risk
  • CISM advises but doesn't own final risk decisions

🔑 Key Terms (hover for definitions)

ISMS GRC SoD Due Care/Diligence Fiduciary Duty Security Charter

⚡ Exam Tips – Domain 1

Always link security decisions to business value and risk
The CISM role is strategic and advisory – avoid answers where CISM makes unilateral decisions
Policies are approved by senior management/board, not IT or security team
When in doubt between governance options, choose the one that best supports business objectives

Information Risk Management
20% of exam · Identify, assess, and respond to information risk
Risk Fundamentals

Risk Formula & Concepts

  • Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Asset Value
  • Threat: potential cause of unwanted incident
  • Vulnerability: weakness that can be exploited
  • Exposure: extent of loss when risk event occurs
  • Residual Risk = Inherent Risk − Controls applied
Risk Assessment

Qualitative vs Quantitative

  • Qualitative: High/Med/Low ratings, subjective, faster
  • Quantitative: ALE = ARO × SLE (in dollar amounts)
  • SLE = Asset Value × Exposure Factor
  • ARO = Annual Rate of Occurrence
  • ALE = Annual Loss Expectancy (quantitative basis)
Risk Response Options

The 4 T's

  • Treat (Mitigate): implement controls to reduce risk
  • Transfer: insurance, contracts (shifts financial impact)
  • Tolerate (Accept): risk within appetite, no action
  • Terminate (Avoid): stop the activity causing the risk
  • Risk acceptance must be formally documented
Risk Appetite & Tolerance

Key Distinctions

  • Risk Appetite: amount of risk an org WANTS to take
  • Risk Tolerance: acceptable variation around appetite
  • Risk Capacity: maximum risk the org CAN absorb
  • Risk Threshold: point at which action must be taken
  • Set by senior management / Board
Risk Register

Documentation & Tracking

  • Records identified risks, their ratings, and responses
  • Owner assigned for each risk item
  • Reviewed regularly – living document
  • Includes inherent and residual risk ratings
  • Key tool for communicating risk to management
Third-Party Risk

Vendor & Supply Chain Risk

  • Risk doesn't disappear when outsourced
  • Contracts must include security requirements
  • Right to audit clause is important
  • SOC 2 reports used to assess vendor controls
  • Ongoing monitoring – not just onboarding

🔑 Key Terms

SLE ALE ARO Exposure Factor Inherent Risk Residual Risk Control Risk

⚡ Exam Tips – Domain 2

Risk acceptance decisions are ALWAYS made by business owners, not the security team
Memorise ALE = ARO × SLE and SLE = Asset Value × EF for calculation questions
Outsourcing transfers financial liability but NOT accountability or responsibility
Risk assessment should happen BEFORE selecting controls

Information Security Program Development & Management
33% of exam · LARGEST domain · Design, build, and manage security program
Program Development

Building the Security Program

  • Start with current-state assessment (gap analysis)
  • Define target state based on risk and business goals
  • Roadmap: prioritised projects to close gaps
  • Security architecture aligns with enterprise architecture
  • Program must be sustainable and measurable
Controls Framework

Control Categories

  • Preventive: stop incidents occurring (firewalls, MFA)
  • Detective: identify when incidents occur (SIEM, logs)
  • Corrective: restore after incident (backup, patching)
  • Deterrent: discourage attackers (warnings, policies)
  • Compensating: alternative when primary control fails
Data Classification

Classification & Handling

  • Owner classifies, Custodian protects
  • Common: Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted
  • Govt: Unclassified, Sensitive, Secret, Top Secret
  • Controls applied must match classification level
  • Data lifecycle: Create → Use → Archive → Destroy
Security Architecture

Defence in Depth Layers

  • Perimeter → Network → Host → Application → Data
  • Zero Trust: never trust, always verify
  • Least privilege: minimum necessary access
  • Separation of duties: no single point of control
  • Need-to-know: access only to what's required
Security Awareness

Training & Culture

  • Awareness: know threats exist (phishing simulations)
  • Training: how to perform specific tasks securely
  • Education: in-depth understanding (for security staff)
  • Tailored by role: executives, developers, general staff
  • Effectiveness measured, not just completion rates
Identity & Access Mgmt

IAM Key Concepts

  • Provisioning and de-provisioning processes
  • Periodic access reviews / recertification
  • Privileged Access Management (PAM): admin accounts
  • SSO and federation for enterprise environments
  • Joiner-Mover-Leaver lifecycle management
Vulnerability Management

Assessment & Remediation

  • Vulnerability scan: automated, identifies weaknesses
  • Penetration test: simulates attacker, confirms exploitability
  • Patch management: timely, risk-based prioritisation
  • CVSS scores used to prioritise (Critical/High/Med/Low)
  • Remediate → Mitigate → Accept (with documented approval)
Third-Party Management

Vendor Security Requirements

  • Security requirements in contracts / SLAs
  • Right-to-audit clauses in agreements
  • SOC 1 (financial controls) vs SOC 2 (security controls)
  • Type I: design at a point in time; Type II: effectiveness over period
  • Ongoing monitoring, not just initial assessment

⚡ Exam Tips – Domain 3

This is the biggest domain (33%) – invest proportional revision time here
Program must always align to business strategy and risk appetite, not just technical standards
A pen test is NOT the same as a vulnerability scan – pen test confirms exploitability
SOC 2 Type II is preferred over Type I because it covers a period of time (effectiveness), not just design
Awareness ≠ Training ≠ Education – know the difference and when each is appropriate

Information Security Incident Management
30% of exam · Plan, respond, and recover from incidents
Incident Lifecycle

6-Phase Response Process

  • ① Preparation: plans, tools, team, training
  • ② Detection & Analysis: identify & confirm incident
  • ③ Containment: stop the spread (short/long term)
  • ④ Eradication: remove root cause
  • ⑤ Recovery: restore to normal operations
  • ⑥ Lessons Learned: post-incident review (PIR)
Key Plans

IRP vs BCP vs DRP

  • IRP: Incident Response Plan – how to respond to security events
  • BCP: Business Continuity Plan – keep business running
  • DRP: Disaster Recovery Plan – recover IT systems
  • BCP is broader; DRP is a subset of BCP
  • All must be tested regularly, not just documented
RTO, RPO, MTD

Recovery Objectives

  • RTO: Recovery Time Objective – max downtime tolerated
  • RPO: Recovery Point Objective – max data loss tolerated
  • MTD/MTO: Maximum Tolerable Downtime/Outage
  • WRT: Work Recovery Time (restore data/app after failover)
  • RTO + WRT ≤ MTD (must satisfy this relationship)
Evidence & Forensics

Digital Forensics Principles

  • Chain of custody: document handling of all evidence
  • Order of volatility: collect RAM before disk before network
  • Do NOT alter original evidence – use forensic copies
  • Write blockers prevent modification of evidence drives
  • Legal hold: preserve evidence when litigation expected
BCP/DR Testing

Test Types (Least → Most Disruptive)

  • Checklist / Desk Review: review plan accuracy
  • Tabletop Exercise: walkthrough discussion
  • Parallel Test: run DR site alongside production
  • Full Interruption: cut over to DR, full test (most disruptive)
  • Simulation: scenario-based without actual failover
Communication & Escalation

During an Incident

  • Pre-defined escalation paths and contact lists
  • Internal: executive notification for major incidents
  • External: customers, regulators, law enforcement
  • Legal and PR involved for significant breaches
  • Breach notification timelines vary by regulation (e.g. GDPR: 72hrs)
Recovery Sites

Hot / Warm / Cold Sites

  • Hot: fully operational, real-time replication, fastest recovery
  • Warm: partially equipped, hours to activate
  • Cold: facility only, days/weeks to bring online
  • Cloud: increasingly used as elastic recovery option
  • Cost inversely related to recovery time
Post-Incident Review

Lessons Learned (PIR)

  • Conduct within 2 weeks while memory is fresh
  • Blameless culture – focus on process improvement
  • Document: timeline, what worked, what didn't
  • Update IRP, BCP, DRP based on findings
  • Share (appropriately) with broader security community

🔑 Key Terms

RTO RPO MTD BIA CSIRT Chain of Custody

⚡ Exam Tips – Domain 4

BIA (Business Impact Analysis) comes BEFORE creating BCP/DRP – you must know the impact first
Containment happens BEFORE eradication – stop the spread, THEN remove the cause
Chain of custody must be maintained – even small breaks can invalidate evidence in court
Remember: RTO + WRT must be less than or equal to MTD
GDPR requires breach notification within 72 hours – a commonly tested regulatory timeline
